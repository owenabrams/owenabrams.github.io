Fear Knows No Time of Day

A FILM BY MICHELE GENTILE
The Project



SYNOPSIS

 
Alone in a strange city, in a strange apartment, in a foreign culture. Alone amongst strangers. Alone with memories that will not relent. Memories of the murder of her mother and father, of her friends and neighbors. Memories of soldiers with guns, of the family house in flames, of a familiar world coming to a devastating end.

Memories of being forced to carry a weapon, to kill. Forced to become one of them, with no hope of return to a normal life. Memories of a rape, and seeing the rapist's face everyday in the face of her own child.

Welcome to Malia's world, the heroine of "Malia".

A heroine she is without question, even though she would never see herself that way. She is ready to face the unimaginable horrors of her past in the witness box at the International Criminal Court. She is ready to see her tormentors held accountable before the whole world for their deeds.

But there is something in the way: her own immeasurable fear, the shadows of the past, her memories, her complicity in the deaths of others. The monster-like creature of her fear, guilt and despair allows her barely no rest. Even the bright day is not a safe refuge.

But on the contrary... Fear knows no time of day...

INTENTIONS

FILM MAKER'S INTENTION
MICHELE GENTILE | SCREENWRITER & DIRECTOR
              
After completion of my first feature-length documentary, "THE COURT," I remained fascinated with the fate of child soldiers. Much of our research material that we collected for the documentary could not be used because it was too cruel. Even the material we included in the finished film led to violent reactions and protests from our fans. These reactions made me realize how absolutely unimaginable the experiences of child soldiers are to Western and European audiences. I started thinking about how to help the general public understand and even experience this fear through film.
The courage of witnesses at the International Criminal Court has never ceased to amaze me. They find
themselves in the foreign environment of another continent, in Europe, with only a short time to learn how to cope with the full horror that revisiting their experiences brings back to life. The experience of examination and cross-examination in court, before Judges, court officials and the defendant him or herself adds an incredible amount of pressure. What more can illustrate the challenge these former child soldiers confront than having to face their tormentors, face what was done to them and what they were made to do, in a past that is never truly past?

Through my research and the shooting of "THE COURT" I have gained understanding as to how witnesses are looked after during their stay in The Hague and how they live. On this basis, I feel comfortable to convey accurately and with great empathy the story of a single child soldier's journey through the process of seeking justice. This fictional character should reflect the fate of many real-life child soldiers, not only their experiences in combat, but the torture that led up to to their release and which follows them in the months and years to come. In particular, the sexual abuse of children and their fates, tied up with those of the children born of sexual abuse, have moved and shaken me to my core. These young people face a lifelong stigma from their family and society even after their liberation that is hard for them to endure and difficult for others to truly understand; their past is never truly past.

The genre of the thriller film draws large audiences; as a category of popular culture, it is as popular as comedies. I believe it is the perfect vehicle to introduce a general audience to the work of the International Criminal Court and the fate of child soldiers. The all too human stories can get lost in the intricacies of the difficult and lengthy procedures of the International Criminal Court; it is important to retrieve the individuals concerned from that maze and lift them up for public view and understanding. I believe that viewers will immediately identify with our heroine. The cinematic approach will enhance their sense of connection with her; from the first frames of the film until the closing credits, she will always be present on screen, front and center, or in the margins. She will no longer be invisible, unseen and uncounted. Read more . . .

WRITER'S INTENTION
CHRISTIANE GENTILE | CO-SCREENWRITER
In May 2013, my son Michele Gentile and his co-director Marcus Vetter released their documentary "THE COURT." I had the opportunity to track the film's development since autumn of 2012. Before then, I only knew of the fate of child soldiers through occasional media coverage. But now I was faced with the unimaginably intense fate of these children. Through preparatory materials for the film, I saw footage of cruelty to children, as well as the destruction of villages and the murder of their inhabitants. I will never forget the image of vultures eating the dead. Read more . . .
              
In reflecting on these things I saw and learned, I agreed on the need to address the fate of child soldiers in a feature film. We wanted, through the action of a popular film to create a greater identification of the spectator with the actor, built on a well-considered movie plot and on sound documentary material and evidence. Our intention is to reach the widest possible audience and to highlight the incredible horrors to which these children are exposed. The genre of thrilling horror seemed a natural fit in this regard. Our aim is to make a psychological thriller that draws traditional lovers of the genre; making a film that contributes to the genre on its own merits is one goal. The other is to share something new with viewers that makes them think a little differently about the real world by the end of the film. It is important to understand that the horror is not meant to be gratuitous or overly bloody or gory.

PRODUCER'S INTENTION
JENNIFER SCHENSE | CO-SCREENWRITER
              
I come to the process of making "Malia" rather circuitously, having started not as a filmmaker but as an international lawyer, having studied first the Russian language and Russian area studies, then law, in particular international and human rights law. I spent six years working for the International NGO Coalition for the International Criminal Court (CICC), followed by nearly twelve years in the ICC Office of the Prosecutor in The Hague, serving mostly as an International Cooperation Adviser and in that capacity, opening doors for the investigations in Darfur and Libya.
My work has taken me all over Africa and the Middle East, as well as frequently to New York to the United Nations with the Prosecutor, to brief the UN Security Council. In the course of working for the ICC Office of the Prosecutor, I have also completed a PhD at the University of Leiden on whether international crimes can be prevented, have begun blogging on Tumblr about prevention-related issues, and have acted as the Lead Editor and a Senior Consultant to the International Academy Nuremberg Principles in the production of a book on deterrence and international courts and tribunals, to be launched at an event in November 2016 in Nuremberg, commemorating the 70th anniversary of the Nuremberg Trials, and thereafter at the ICC Assembly of States Parties in The Hague in mid-November 2016. PRODUCER'S INTENTION
FLORENCE OLARA | PRODUCER
              
I am Florence Olara. I am originally from Uganda and spent my early years in a number of countries in the Great Lakes region of Africa, fleeing wars. Eventually, members of my family sought refuge in the United Kingdom. Despite my back-to-back experience of war and being a child refugee multiple times, my memorable experience was also witnessing humans being human and acting humanely towards one
another, trying to help those in need whenever they could. This experience led me to the field of international criminal justice and working at the International Criminal Court because I believe the Court is a key tool in eventually breaking the cycle of violence around the world.

A few years ago Jennifer Schense told me about an idea she had to create the House of Nuremberg, a company that would produce a number of materials including film and books, to highlight global issues. I loved the idea. As someone who experienced war multiple times, the need to highlight global issues is closer to me than most. To help us in this process, we also enlisted the help of German filmmaker Michele Gentile who also strongly believes in using film as a medium to highlight global issues and educate the masses.

I am the Chair of the Peace, Justice and Security Foundation in the establishment of the House of Nuremberg. Even when removed from where Malia experienced the terrible atrocity of being a child soldier, the horror of what she experienced travels with her. Her battle in fighting this inner demon is not just for herself but for many other victims just like her. This is why I have committed the Foundation to this project. Read more . . .

PRODUCER'S INTENTION
HOLGER BERGMANN | PRODUCER
              
Child soldiers - a topic which is very far away for us in Western Europe. One sometimes hears this term in the news, but hardly anyone is aware of the reality it represents. How does a child become a perpetrator? How much suffering does a child endure in this transformation, being forced to kill, just to save his or her own life? How can this horrible transformation end in justice? Can the clock be turned back?
Michele and Christiane Gentile dare to tackle this topic and to put the startling true story of child soldiers at the heart of their film, Malia. Against the background of the real-life conviction of the DRC militia leader Thomas Lubanga by the International Criminal Court in The Hague, the story of Malia emerged, based on true statements, narrations and memories of the victims and witnesses surrounding this case.

Even ten years after Malia's cruel childhood experiences, fear is her constant companion. The filmgoer accompanies her on a journey through her memories - the violent death of her parents, and the choice either to be slaughtered senselessly or to be part of a bestial war machine as a child soldier. Mistreatment, rape, and the struggle for survival - her gun is the only belonging she retains. After years, Malia comes face to face with the spirit that has become the symbol of her suffering: her former militia leader, against whom she will now testify, at great personal sacrifice, before the International Criminal Court.

I find it important to tell stories that show how essential it is not to hide but to face the past with bravery. Only in this way can there be justice instead of forgetting; only in this way can we prevent future horrors. Read more . . .

HOUSE OF NUREMBURG

The House is a for-profit company incorporated in The Hague, the Netherlands, whose mission is to explore, elaborate on and popularize concepts of justice, building on the experiences of the Nuremberg Trials and on the history of Nuremberg more generally. The name House of Nuremberg, in particular, evokes an image of a common living and working space, and conveys the idea that all are welcome within the same house, that all are welcome to come to Nuremberg to contribute. This is particularly important in building not only on the Nuremberg trial experience, but also in answering to the other legacy of Nuremberg, that of the race laws, prejudice, and exclusionism. The idea of the House of Nuremberg is meant to convey that everyone is welcome.

The House of Nuremberg is established as a for-profit institution, similar to a house of fashion, to develop projects and collaborations in the fields of film, writing and publishing to start, with possible extension into fashion, architecture and other fields of art, once the House is firmly established. The House of Nuremberg's initial products will be in the field of film and potentially television.

The House of Nuremberg is established as a for-profit institution for two reasons: first, to emphasize that products elaborating on concepts of justice in the fields of film, television, publishing and other fields of art can be profitable and do not require a strictly charitable approach to their production and distribution. Likewise, creating and sustaining space for dialogue about justice does not require the framework of a nonprofit or non-governmental organization. And second, to emphasize that the House of Nuremberg itself can be financially self-sustaining. It is the goal of the House's founders for the House of Nuremberg to become a serious, well-recognized, mainstream production house, with the financial capacity to support the long-term popularization of both fictional and documentary films, worldwide. Toward this end, it will produce films and other products in-house, as well as acquiring films from established and promising new filmmakers who have produced outstanding films but who do not have the capacity to ensure their distribution. The House will appeal to forward-thinking financiers who recognize the value of excellence in both fictional and documentary filmmaking and other artistic products on justice issues, both from the perspective of the dialogue they can create and from the perspective of their value as investments. Finally, the goal for the House of Nuremberg is to be in a position to financially support other initiatives that serve the vision and goals of the House. For this reason, the House of Nuremberg will donate a portion of its profits to charitable ventures that represent the spirit and ethic of the House, including through supporting the work of the Peace, Justice and Security Foundation. It will endeavour to do so in a way that draws attention to worthwhile causes at a time intended to consolidate their further support, worldwide.

BRIEF SUMMARY

Malia's world is not easy to enter. She has experienced things that are hard to describe and even more difficult to understand, but we invite the filmgoer to stand with her nonetheless and to see the world through her eyes. Malia has been a child soldier, enslaved, abused, and threatened constantly with death for years. How can a viewer truly understand and experience this, in such a way that is exciting and challenging, and ultimately hopeful?

And where is justice? The International Criminal Court - the ICC in The Hague - is fighting for this on behalf of the world community. At this place, committed people work to ensure that the greatest criminals of our time do not get away with impunity. Malia is here to help. Her testimony is essential to bring her former tormentor to justice and ultimately to put him in prison.

Malia is not alone. Her attorney Alice and her guardian Antonia from the witness protection program of the ICC stand by her side. CHARACTERS

MALIA NASUNA
is the film's protagonist. She is a young woman of 23 years, who comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo. During the Second Congo War in 2002-2003, she was stolen from her home at the age of 13 and forced to join the private army of Thomas Lubanga Dyilo, a self-proclaimed militia leader, to fight as a child soldier. Her parental home was destroyed in the course of her abduction, and she was forced to witness the murder of her parents and the disappearance of her siblings. A commander of the militia regularly sexually abused Malia from the age of 13 to 14. This abuse led to pregnancy and the birth of her daughter, Jolene. Her left hand is missing her ring finger, which her former commander chopped off as punishment. At the age of 14, Congolese government soldiers liberated Malia from captivity. She moved in with her newborn daughter to the childrens' home of a Christian organization. Since the age of 18, Malia has also worked for the Christian organization. After her liberation, Malia had to have a hysterectomy, as the multiple rapes had so physically damaged her, she could not recover.

ANTONIA JONKER
is 45 years old and Dutch. She is a psychotherapist and lawyer and works in the witness protection program of the International Criminal Court (ICC) as a caregiver. Antonia is a maternal type. She is very gentle with Malia, but can also be very energetic. She is married and has two daughters, ages 22 and 19. Antonia speaks cautiously and in a friendly manner with Malia. She tries to cheer up Malia and to help her to adjust to this temporary Western life. Antonia accompanies Malia to the ICC to give her testimony, and stays with her until Malia boards the plane back to Africa.

ALICE HOFFMANN
is a lawyer, hired by the victims participation and reparation unit to represent Malia as a victim before the court. She takes it upon herself to help Malia refresh her memories in preparation for her testimony in court. She is around 45 years old, slim and although she always remains calm, she can't shake the impact of the terrible things she reads about and deals with, day after day. Alice is idealistic, but also sober and professional. Alice tries to remain calm and collected, as Malia's increasing fears make it harder and harder for her to testify. Alice knows how much of the case rests on Malia's testimony.

JOLENE NASUNA
is 9 years old and follows her mother some time later to The Hague. She is a happy and keen child. Jolene suspects much of what her mother experienced, although they have not really discussed it, as Malia believes she is too young to know. Jolene is intelligent and has a clear idea of her future. She wants to be a pilot. She is living together with her mother at a home run by a Christian organisation in Uganda and is going to school there. Her English skills are very good. Jolene has become a kind of caregiver for her traumatized mother. She arrives two days before Malia’s testimony in The Hague.





ODONGO KALANZI
is a six-year-old boy, whom Malia encountered during a raid on his village. Malia had tried to warn away Odongo during the raid. The commander however noticed this and tried to force Malia to shoot Odongo. Malia refused, and finally, the commander himself shot Odongo in front of Malia.

MALIA'S MOTHER JOLENE
was killed during the raid when the militia stole her daughter. In a dreamlike vision the night before Malia's testimony, she appears to Malia, singing in support with other family members from the village.

THE BEING
is a reflection of Malia. The monster-like creature represents in essence the materialization of Malia’s fears and experiences, which she has until now kept hidden inside her. It has a terrible tooth mouth, a skeletal body and long bony legs. It only appears to Malia in the apartment. Each foot has three long claws, which can be heard clicking against the floor of the apartment when it walks around. Although by nature, it has little physical substance and moves in an unnatural and edgy way, it appears to be of heavy weight, and runs like a large predator. The muting of all background noise presages its appearance, and scares Malia so badly, she can barely move. It feeds on Malia’s fears, in particular her fear that her experiences have made her somehow a monster, that this part of her will never leave her. Only with Jolene's arrival, does it gradually lose its power and substance. But it is never truly gone.

Malia's Apartment


When we began planning for this film, we envisioned a flat that would fit Malia's needs and would berealistic, in accordance with the ICC's practices. After detailed research on various locations throughout The Hague, we realized that we would need to build the flat in a production studio, to be able to move walls and realize the unique and challenging camera moves that will define this film's identity. To better understand how we envision this process unfolding, we have produced a 3D model of the flat with realistic measurements, which allows the viewer to "walk" through it. We view this as yet another step towards bringing this film and Malia's story to life.

MALIA

There is this special smile that Malia cannot forget. The smile of a girl. It is the only thing Malia does not want to forget, from the time in her life she must now recall. This smile has suddenly returned to her. It was dangerous for a girl to smile the first time Malia met her. No one else would have dared. Only this girl had done it and only for her.

In the breaks between fighting every day, the rifles had to be cleaned. Malia had guarded her rifle like nothing else. It was the only thing she had succeeded in guarding, as her body, her soul and her spirit had had to survive without protection, her soul crushed, her spirit dulled, her body raped. And then, in the midst of this abyss, as she was cleaning her rifle, there had come this person, robbed from the lap of her family, like her, with that smile. They became girlfriends without words and companions in horror without thinking or hesitating. One was for the other. ... Here Film Begins

We encounter Malia for the first time. She just arrived in The Hague. Antonia picked her up from the airport and took her to the apartment. She turned on the small lamp, which stands beside Malia's bed on a small night stand, that Malia likes well. The light of the lamp is very bright. Her hands are clearly visible in its glow. They are wrinkled and very black in contrast to the apartment with its light colors. The walls are white, the bedding is white, even the carpet has the color of cooked milk. Her left ring finger is missing. A small stunted stump reminds her of the unimaginable pain she experienced when it was hacked off in punishment because she could not stand to watch her dead friend being eaten by vultures. She stops herself from thinking further of her friend.

She lifts her head. It seems to her as if the whole mysterious continent on which she now finds herself is represented in this apartment. Europe. She must look more closely. "This is your home for now," Antonia had said. A home for now, what is that supposed to mean, Malia wonders? She is now alone. She slowly gets up and walks through the rooms. In her village in her hut there was only a large room for everything. Here, there is a room for everything. In the living room she finds shelves full of books, that remind her of her home with the sisters in Africa. Finally she spies something familiar. Malia loves books. She takes one whose title she likes, "MOMO". She wants to start reading immediately, but finds herself distracted by the water faucet, by the ready abundance of water, as well as electricity, at any time of the day or night.

Before Malia goes to sleep, Antonia calls again to check if she is ok. Her voice sounds kind and friendly, a warm, human sound breaking the silence.
She seems nice. Malia falls asleep, thinking on how much effort has gone into bringing her here. Suddenly she is almost happy that Antonia is coming tomorrow. She will see the city, the city whose streets Van Gogh also trod. She sleeps under thick blankets instead of a mosquito net. At home, the nurses would never allow her to sleep without a mosquito net. After turning off the bedside light, deep darkness spreads. This is too much for Malia. She turns on the light again. The electricity is still there. She switches the light on and off a few more times. Finally, her exhausted mind takes over, and Malia falls asleep in the dark.

LOOK AND FEEL







     
 

        
 How can claustrophobia, loneliness, and recollection of the past be made manifest through the experience
of suspense and horror, in deep contrast to the comfort of this cozy and quiet apartment? Malia's terror comes
ever closer through the use of light and sound effects, and unusually long, planned sequences, culminating
in the appearance of the monster-like creature. The way of creating suspense in the film is comparable with
films like "The sixth Sense", "Signs" or "Poltergeist".


The use of different color schemes will assist the audience in understanding Malia's recollections and resulting
state of mind. There will be no flashbacks or cinematic returns to Africa, but rather Africa will materialize in
the rooms of her apartment. It will emerge from the elements of her surroundings: from the walls, ceilings,
doors, floors, and furniture. Fire and violence will break out inside her temporary home in The Hague, at least
in her remembrance and imagination.


Through special camera work and the application of image design, color, and the economical use of sound and
music, the film will bring back Malia's life and experiences. Many of these scenes will take place with almost
no sound, to illustrate her solitude and the suffocating nature of her experiences. For Malia, again there is no
escape, and a closed door offers no protection. No one else can hear or see anything of the creature.


Malia's strong character emerges in her ability not only to overcome the cruelty she has experienced, but also
to raise her child with love. But there are cracks in her facade of self-composure and calm. How can she live
with herself? How can she look into her own eyes? The mirror is an important touchstone and tool in the
horror genre. The intention here is to find new approaches to this familiar object, and to defy the viewer's
expectations in this regard. Where thrillers traditionally play on a primal fear of the dark and the ability to
sleep in safety, this film addresses crimes that take place in the light of day. Bright daylight offers no refuge for
Malia, and serves to demonstrate how much courage Malia needs to muster to give her testimony and relive
that unbearable experience.


To intensify her feelings to the audience we will use unusual multiplication of her real world sourroundings
such as the busy street life.

IMPRESSIONS






 



            
The process of scriptwriting took Michele Gentile and Christiane Gentile more than two and a half years. Drawing out this story took them from tears to euphoria. Both of them emphasized especially the importance for them of creating believable dialogues and paying extra attention to helping the viewer to understand the character of Malia. The script comprises 131 pages and is completely written following International standards in both English and German.

          





            
After we finished the script, we redoubled our efforts to spread the word about this project to our friends and partners. We recruited well-known German actress Katja Riemann (e.g. "Comedian Harmonists", 1997 or "He is Back", 2015) and international director of photography Jorg Widmer (e.g. "V for Vendetta", 2005 or "Inglorious Basterds", 2009) who penned letters of intent to the producers of Malia, and who continue to consult on casting, financing, production and other crucial matters.

          





            
In planning for the production of Malia, we undertook our first location scouting of the canal street in The Hague where our heroine Malia lives. We also visited the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam. We wanted to envision the film's story unfolding in these cities and surroundings. We even found the supermarket where we will shoot an important scene with Malia and a boy who reminds her of Odongo.

          





            
In this process, our documentary filmmaker Jannik Mayer has tailed us, in order to record the process of producing Malia from start to finish.

          



        
      

 

    



 
    

    
 
 






FILM'S INTENTIONS AND GOALS










      

        
An international organization, the International Criminal Court (ICC) is specifically designed to deal
the most serious crimes of concern to the international community. Although the idea that a permanent international criminal court was strongly needed, and therefore, should be created, had lingered for a very long time, the realization of that idea has come about quite recently. In particular, concerns over sovereign rights of States made them reluctant to discuss the issue until 1995, when the negotiation process began at the UN. In July 1998, at the Rome Diplomatic Conference, 120 states adopted the Rome Statute establishing the International Criminal Court and opened it for signature. After the required number of 60 ratifications was reached, the Rome Statute, along with the ICC, officially entered into force in July 2002.


However, the Court did not become fully functional until 2003, when the states parties elected the first Judges and independent Prosecutor for the Court. After that, the ICC began operating in its headquarters in The Hague, "the capital of international peace and justice".


The Court is dealing with the gravest crimes, that is, genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes and eventually the crime of aggression, which will be added the Rome Statute in 2017. At first sight, it might seem that the list is too narrow. However, there are many crimes that fit within these categories, such as murder, rape, forced prostitution, destruction of cultural property, taking hostages, enslavement, torture, and many others (see articles 6 through 8 of the Rome Statute). Furthermore, the inclusion of aggression as a crime to be dealt with by the Court on the list is an important step in preventing future conflicts.


It is nothing less than "small" revolution in international law and global politics, what the Times of India called in 1998 "international lawmaking of historic proportions".


In 2013, network television viewers received a taste of the International Criminal Court through NBC's new police series, "CROSSING LINES". The show follows a fictional "special crimes unit" of the ICC tasked with investigating murders, kidnappings, bank heists, and other crimes. Unfortunately, the show's storyline bears little resemblance to the mandate or work of the real ICC. It would not pursue the types of crimes portrayed in the show. Yet, the show's creation, like the film "THE INTERPRETER" with Nicole Kidman and Sean Penn, indicates growing popular awareness of the ICC's increasing global importance.


More can and should be done to raise awareness of this institution, and to encourage discussion about how it can best contribute to global peace, justice and security.


This film is based with documentary precision on facts and figures relating to the ICC's work and the actual experience of child soldiers. The goal is to help the audience identify with the experience of a former child soldier. Her fate is a reflection of many true child soldiers' fates and is reinforced constantly by staying close to our protagonist. We make the audience feel Malia's fears, get to know her past and make them see what she is seeing.


This film may be an important signpost to people and comunities all around the world. The goal is to spread the importance of the ICC and to evaluate and to illuminate its work internationally. No person on this planet should ever be subjected to such forces and brutalities.


Our goal is to implement this movie only if the funds are sufficient. A film with such significance must either be completely and properly done or not done at all. A poorly conceived production in no way meets the farreaching effect that is desperately needed on this subject or may even harm the reputation of the ICC.


Of course, it is quite clear that the success of this film is entirely based on the acting skills of our main characters. Especially our leading lady must be able to cover a range of emotions in a precise and convincing way; her portrayal must enchant the audience and bring them to her side from the start. Her credibility is more important than anything else.


We as a team are looking forward to this challenge and are ready to undertake all the necessary steps to shoot and promote this movie as conscientiously as possible.


Thank you for reading our treatment. We hope we have caught your attention and your imagination, and that you are as hooked on the story as we are. We would be happy to hear your thoughts and comments on this.


All the very best!
